\subsection{Fundamentals of Random Matrix}\label{subsec:fundamentals-of-random-matrix}

Random Matrix Theory (RMT) bridges linear algebra and probability theory\supercite{vivo_random_2017}, examining the statistical behavior of matrices with randomly distributed elements.
It allows to find order in chaos, revealing underlying structures in complex systems like biological networks\supercite{deng_molecular_2012}, financial markets\supercite{pharasi_complex_2018}, and quantum physics\supercite{guhr_random_1997}.
A key focus of RMT is to understand the properties of their eigenvalues of the matrices with random entries.
\\\\
\noindent TA basic calculation in RMT is finding the spacing distribution of eigenvalues.
A simple example involves a 2x2 real symmetric matrix with Gaussian random variables as entries.

\noindent Consider the matrix $X$:
\begin{gather*}
    X=\begin{pmatrix}
x_1 & x_3\\ x_3 & x_2
\end{pmatrix}\\
    x_1, x_2 \sim N(0,1) \quad x_3 \sim N(0,\frac{1}{2})\\
\end{gather*}


\noindent Where $N(0,1)$ denotes a Gaussian distribution with mean 0 and variance 1 and $N(0,\frac{1}{2})$ denotes a Gaussian distribution with mean 0 and variance $\frac{1}{2}$.

The variance of the off-diagonal element is chosen to be half of the variance of the diagonal elements for a specific reason that allows an ea
The question is that we want to find the probability density function (pdf) of the spacing s between the two eigenvalues.
So $s=\lambda_1-\lambda_2$ where $\lambda_1$ and $\lambda_2$ are the eigenvalues of the matrix $X$.

\noindent This spacing for a 2x2 matrix can be calculated like this:
\[s = \lambda_1 - \lambda_2 = \sqrt{(x_1 - x_2)^2 + 4x_3^2}\]

\noindent We're gonna skip the whole demonstration of the calculation, but the final equation for the pdf of the spacing s is:
\[P(s)=\frac{s}{2}e^{-\frac{s^2}{4}}\]

Despite its simplicity, this result is remarkably profound: it reveals that the probability of sampling two eigenvalues that are "very close" to each other (as $s \to 0$) is extremely low.
It's as though each eigenvalue "senses" the presence of the others and adjusts to maintain a certain distance—neither too close nor too far.
This behavior is reminiscent of birds perched on an electric wire or cars parked along a street: maintaining a balance between proximity and spacing. \supercite{livan_introduction_2017}
\\\\
In biological systems, the transition from global network interactions, characterized by the Wigner's surmise distribution, to isolated modular structures, described by the Poisson distribution, reflects the emergence of functional modularity.
This transition highlights level repulsion, where eigenvalues tend to "avoid" close proximity.
The phenomenon underscores intrinsic correlations among eigenvalues, even when matrix entries are independently distributed.

\noindent By investigating eigenvalue spacing distributions, researchers identify key properties like the Wigner's surmise distribution in GOE systems or the exponential decay of Poisson distributions in decoupled systems.
These techniques offer a powerful framework for exploring complex systems, including biological networks and beyond.\supercite{luo_application_2006}

Random Matrix Theory allow us to have a better understanding of the organization of complex systems by analyzing eigenvalue spacings, capturing transitions from global interactions to modular structures.
This approach underscores the utility of statistical models like Wigner’s surmise and Poisson distributions in exploring biological networks and other interconnected systems.

\subsection{Applications of Random Matrix Theory in Many-body Systems}\label{subsec:applications-of-random-matrix-theory-in-many-body-systems}

By describing the statistical properties of spectra in complex quantum systems, RMT bridges seemingly disparate phenomena through its universal principles.
The role of RMT in understanding many-body systems, its implications for quantum chaos, and its connections to field theory and statistical mechanics, allow us to understand the versatility and foundational importance of it in modern physics.

Many-body systems encompass complex structures involving numerous particles interacting via two-body forces.
Examples include atomic nuclei, which consist of nucleons bound by strong nuclear forces, and atoms and molecules, where ions and electrons interact through electromagnetic forces.
These systems demonstrate high levels of complexity.
The Hamiltonian is an operator which determines the evolution of a quantum state through the Schrödinger equation.
It's described for $N$ particules like :
\[\hat{H}=\sum^N_{n=1}\hat{T}_n+\hat{V}\]
Where $\hat{T}_n$ is the kinetic energy operator of particle n and $\hat{V}$ is the potential energy function.
The key idea is to replace the complex, specific Hamiltonian of the system with an ensemble of random matrices that share the same symmetries.
\\\\
At low incident energies, the use of the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) in modeling compound nucleus scattering assumes that the nucleus equilibrates internally faster than it decays.
However, as incident energy increases, the decay time becomes comparable to the equilibration time, meaning the nucleus can decay before full equilibration.

\noindent To address this, the model is extended using the nuclear shell model, dividing the compound nucleus into classes of states with fixed particle-hole numbers.
Each class is represented by a random matrix, and the coupling between neighborefers to the absence of a fermion in an energy level it would occupy in the ground statering classes, governed by the two-body interaction, is also modeled using random matrices.
Imagine sorting all possible quantum states of a system (like a nucleus) into different groups based on their particle-hole number.
This means each group contains states with the same number of particles excited above the ground state and the same number of holes left behind.
Each of these groups is then modeled using a random matrix.

Instead of trying to calculate the exact energy values for each state in a group, which is extremely slow and complex and laborious, we use a random matrix to represent the overall statistical behavior of the energy levels within that group.
The random matrix is chosen from an ensemble that respects the symmetries of the physical system.
For instance, if the system possesses time-reversal symmetry, a random matrix ensemble like the Gaussian Orthogonal Ensemble (GOE) would be appropriate.
The total Hamiltonian is then a band matrix whose entries are random matrices.
By doing this, RMT enables the calculation of statistical properties of the S-matrix, providing a deeper understanding of the behavior of resonances and the fluctuations in cross-sections.
